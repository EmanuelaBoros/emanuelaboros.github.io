LLMs in archives: fluent, useful, suspicious

Draft note · replace date

I am interested in LLMs in archives because they are genuinely useful and genuinely suspicious. They can summarize, reformulate, search, translate, and help people move through collections that are too large to read manually. But their fluency creates a problem: a good sentence can look like evidence even when it is only a performance of confidence.

For historical and cultural heritage collections, this matters. A generated answer should not detach itself from the document, the image, the page, the date, the OCR quality, the uncertainty, or the historical context. I do not want systems that only sound right. I want systems that show where they are standing.

Note

Documents are evidence, not just data

Draft note · replace date

Calling documents “data” is useful, but it also hides something. Documents are traces of institutions, bodies, decisions, machines, errors, power, memory, and bureaucracy. When we process them automatically, we are not only extracting text. We are rearranging evidence.

This is why I care about traceability. If an AI system extracts a person, a place, a relation, or an event, I want to know where it came from, how damaged the source was, what alternatives existed, and how much the model is guessing.

Fragment

Noise is not always a bug

Draft fragment · replace date

Noise is often treated as something to remove before the “real” work begins. But in historical collections, noise can tell us about scanning practices, paper quality, typography, language change, archival decisions, and the violence of compression into machine-readable form.

The question is not only how to clean the document. The question is what kind of document remains after cleaning, and what kind of history becomes easier — or harder — to see.
